# Stellar Princesses — Product Requirements Document

**Document status:** Full-vision product definition
**Product type:** Single-player science-fantasy role-playing game
**Working title:** Stellar Princesses
**Purpose:** Define the intended product in totality without prescribing implementation

---

## 1. Product Concept

Stellar Princesses is a character-driven science-fantasy RPG about Princess Lyra Solari, heir to an interstellar sovereignty whose source of authority and protection—the Celestial Crown—has been shattered by an incursion from beyond reality. Its fragments have scattered across a wounded frontier. Lyra must leave the safety of her royal home, assemble a found family drawn from distinct alien cultures, reclaim the Crown Shards, and decide what kind of ruler—and what kind of power—the galaxy actually needs.

The game combines the warmth and legibility of a classic town-centered adventure with strategic party combat, exploration-driven discovery, expressive companion relationships, equipment and ability growth, personal quests, and multi-phase boss encounters. Its central fantasy is not simply becoming stronger. It is growing from a protected heir into a capable leader whose greatest power comes from trust, responsibility, and the people willing to stand beside her.

The tone should balance wonder, danger, humor, melancholy, and triumph. The visual and audio identity should evoke a playable storybook from a distant future: royal silhouettes, alien cultures, luminous cosmic phenomena, intimate character moments, and dramatic transformations presented with crisp, readable stylization.

The product may retain, expand, reinterpret, or fully rewrite any existing content in service of this vision. Existing names, structures, mechanics, and story beats are foundations rather than constraints unless explicitly identified as core requirements in this document.

## 2. Product Vision

### 2.1 Player Promise

The player will:

- Begin as a sheltered but determined princess and feel her grow into a formidable, self-defined leader.
- Build a party of memorable companions whose personalities, histories, and relationships matter in and out of combat.
- Explore a connected science-fantasy frontier where each place has its own culture, conflict, ecology, and secret.
- Make meaningful choices about leadership, loyalty, sacrifice, and the use of extraordinary power.
- Master a strategic combat system in which party composition, timing, synergy, preparation, and adaptation all matter.
- Return repeatedly to a home base that changes in response to the journey and feels worth protecting.
- Earn spectacular evolutions that are mechanical, visual, emotional, and narrative milestones.
- Confront bosses that feel like dramatic tests of understanding rather than simple health checks.
- Reach an ending that reflects the player’s relationships, decisions, discoveries, and philosophy of rule.

### 2.2 Experience Statement

Stellar Princesses should feel like gathering a strange and lovable royal court while crossing a luminous, endangered galaxy. The player should alternate between comfort and peril: returning home to familiar faces, venturing into the unknown, surviving escalating threats, learning who their companions really are, and gradually gaining enough power to reshape the future.

### 2.3 Design Pillars

1. **Princess power fantasy with earned transformation**
   Royal identity, elegance, courage, and cosmic power are central. Strength must be earned through action, accountability, and growth.

2. **Companions are the heart of the journey**
   Party members are complete characters with agency, personal stakes, evolving relationships, and distinct play styles.

3. **Readable strategy with expressive depth**
   Combat must be understandable at a glance while supporting meaningful combinations, counterplay, experimentation, and mastery.

4. **A living home worth saving**
   The home base is an emotional anchor, a social space, a progression space, and a visible record of the player’s impact.

5. **Wonder through cohesive science fantasy**
   Technology, royalty, magic-like cosmic forces, alien biology, and the Void must belong to one internally coherent world.

6. **Bosses as narrative and mechanical climaxes**
   Major encounters must test what the player learned, reveal character or world truth, and leave a lasting impression.

7. **Choice without premature closure**
   The product should support multiple valid play styles and interpretations. Open questions should remain fertile design space until playtesting or narrative development provides a reason to decide.

## 3. Product Goals and Non-Goals

### 3.1 Product Goals

- Deliver a complete, emotionally satisfying role-playing campaign with a clear beginning, escalating middle, and consequential ending.
- Establish Stellar Princesses as a distinctive world capable of supporting future stories without making future content necessary for this game to feel complete.
- Make every core companion useful, likable, narratively relevant, and mechanically differentiated.
- Ensure progression changes how the player thinks and plays, not only the size of numerical values.
- Support both story-motivated players and systems-motivated players without forcing either group to engage deeply with every optional layer.
- Make exploration consistently rewarding through story, utility, discovery, challenge, or expression.
- Provide a polished experience across the intended range of supported input methods and display contexts.
- Respect the player’s time through clear information, low-friction navigation, flexible difficulty, and reliable saving.

### 3.2 Non-Goals

- Competitive multiplayer is not required for the full vision.
- Always-online play is not required.
- Live-service retention structures, recurring monetization, and artificial engagement loops are not part of the core product.
- Photorealism is not required; clarity, personality, and cohesion take priority.
- Infinite procedural content is not required; authored meaning takes priority over volume.
- A single canonical play style, party composition, moral answer, or character build is not desired.

### 3.3 Open Product Possibilities

The following remain valid concept spaces and must not be foreclosed by the core design:

- Additional campaigns, worlds, companions, playable protagonists, or eras.
- New Game Plus, challenge modes, boss rematches, or postgame stories.
- Localization, expanded platform support, and alternate control interfaces.
- Optional cooperative or social features that do not compromise the single-player experience.
- Expanded character customization, relationship expression, or user-generated challenge formats.
- Adaptation of the visual, technical, or content pipeline as the final scope becomes clear.

## 4. Audience and Positioning

### 4.1 Primary Audience

- Players who enjoy character-driven JRPGs and approachable tactical RPGs.
- Players attracted to science fantasy, royal transformation, alien companions, and found-family stories.
- Players who value exploration, party building, progression, and memorable bosses.
- Players who appreciate retro-inspired presentation with modern usability.

### 4.2 Secondary Audience

- Narrative-focused players who prefer adjustable challenge and strong character arcs.
- Systems-focused players who enjoy buildcraft, synergy, optional challenges, and optimization.
- Younger or less experienced RPG players drawn by visual clarity and an inviting premise.
- Players seeking inclusive heroic fantasy without cynicism as the default emotional register.

### 4.3 Intended Rating and Content Boundaries

The target is a broadly accessible teen audience, subject to final content review. The game may depict stylized combat, grief, loss, corruption, existential threat, and emotionally difficult choices. It should avoid gratuitous gore, sexualized treatment of young characters, cruelty presented only for spectacle, and ambiguity about player-facing safety options.

## 5. Core Player Journey

### 5.1 Macro Loop

The complete player journey should repeatedly connect:

1. Return to the home base.
2. Learn about new needs, threats, opportunities, and character developments.
3. Prepare the party, equipment, supplies, and objectives.
4. Travel to a destination or choose among available leads.
5. Explore, investigate, solve local problems, and discover optional content.
6. Face escalating encounters and environmental complications.
7. Resolve a major confrontation, decision, or revelation.
8. Gain power, knowledge, resources, relationships, or access.
9. See consequences reflected in companions, communities, and the home base.
10. Choose the next priority.

### 5.2 Moment-to-Moment Loop

Moment-to-moment play should alternate among:

- Traversal and observation.
- Environmental interaction and discovery.
- Character dialogue and choice.
- Tactical combat and recovery.
- Party and inventory decisions.
- Short-form puzzles, hazards, or traversal challenges.
- Rewards that create new options rather than only increasing totals.

### 5.3 Session Shape

The game should support:

- Short sessions that yield a clear accomplishment.
- Medium sessions that complete a quest, dungeon segment, or relationship beat.
- Long sessions that carry the player through a full region arc or major boss.
- Safe stopping points before and after high-intensity sequences.
- Easy reorientation after time away.

## 6. Narrative Requirements

### 6.1 Premise

The Celestial Crown is an ancient source of legitimacy, protection, and transformative power tied to the Stellar Sovereignty. A Voidborn assault shatters it, damages the capital’s security, and scatters its shards across the frontier. Lyra survives the fall but loses the certainty that her title alone can protect anyone. She departs to recover the shards, understand the enemy, gather allies, and determine whether restoration is truly the same as salvation.

The story must interrogate:

- What makes authority legitimate?
- Whether power can be inherited without being examined.
- What responsibility accompanies transformative strength.
- Whether institutions should be restored, reformed, shared, or replaced.
- Whether sacrifice is noble, avoidable, imposed, or chosen.
- How trust changes people who expect to stand alone.

### 6.2 Central Thematic Question

**What does power cost, and who do you become when you claim it?**

This question must appear through character conflict, world history, systemic choices, and endings—not only dialogue.

### 6.3 Story Structure

The campaign must include a complete dramatic arc:

- **Opening: The Fall** — Establish Lyra, the Crown, the home base, the attack, the immediate loss, and the need to act.
- **Departure: The First Claim** — Give the player an early victory that also reveals the danger and ambiguity of Crown power.
- **Expansion: The Gathering** — Open the frontier, introduce major cultures and companions, and allow meaningful prioritization.
- **Complication: The Cost of Restoration** — Reveal that the Crown, the Sovereignty, or the accepted history is incomplete or compromised.
- **Fracture: The Party Tested** — Bring companion goals and Lyra’s duty into genuine conflict.
- **Reckoning: The Void Answered** — Resolve the nature of the Voidborn threat and the consequences of accumulated choices.
- **Decision: The Future Crown** — Require Lyra and the player to decide what power and governance should become.
- **Epilogue: A Changed Galaxy** — Show personal and societal consequences with enough specificity to reward investment.

The exact number of acts, regions, shards, and hours remains open. The final structure must provide sufficient time for each core companion, each major culture, the central mystery, and Lyra’s transformation to develop without padding.

### 6.4 Narrative Agency

Player choices must be:

- Clearly framed without revealing every consequence.
- Consistent with plausible versions of Lyra.
- Reflected in relationships, local outcomes, future dialogue, available support, or endings.
- Resistant to simplistic “good versus evil” labeling where the issue is genuinely complex.
- Reversible only when the fiction supports reconsideration.
- Summarized or recalled when later consequences depend on old decisions.

Not every dialogue choice needs a branching plot. Choices may define tone, values, intimacy, priorities, or self-conception. Major choices must have proportionate consequences.

### 6.5 Ending Requirements

The ending must account for:

- Lyra’s final relationship to the Crown and sovereignty.
- The fate or status of the Voidborn conflict.
- Core companion outcomes and completed personal arcs.
- The condition of major regions and communities.
- The strength and character of the home base.
- Significant optional discoveries or alliances.
- The player’s demonstrated philosophy of leadership.

Multiple endings, a modular epilogue, or a hybrid structure are all viable. No ending should invalidate the majority of the player’s journey merely for surprise. Ambiguity may remain where it deepens the theme or supports future stories, but the main campaign must feel resolved.

### 6.6 Lore and Exposition

- Essential context must be understandable through the main path.
- Optional lore must deepen rather than repair the core story.
- Cultures must be presented through people, places, customs, conflicts, and material life—not only encyclopedia entries.
- The player must have a way to revisit discovered people, places, terms, events, and clues.
- Contradictory accounts may be used intentionally where history is contested.
- Mystery must be paced through questions, partial answers, reversals, and eventual payoff.

### 6.7 Dialogue

- Every major character must have a distinct voice, worldview, and conversational rhythm.
- Dialogue must support player reorientation and avoid unnecessary repetition.
- Companion banter must respond to party composition, location, story state, and relationships.
- Ambient dialogue must evolve as events change.
- Humor must emerge from character and circumstance rather than undermining dramatic stakes.
- Important choices must remain readable and accessible regardless of text speed.

## 7. Character Requirements

### 7.1 Lyra Solari

Lyra is the required protagonist and emotional center.

Her arc must move from sheltered dependence toward self-authored leadership. She begins with courage but limited experience, a public identity defined by inheritance, and an understandable desire to restore what was lost. She must make mistakes, learn through relationships, confront the history of her institution, and become capable of choosing a future that is not merely dictated by tradition.

The player must be able to shape:

- Her conversational tone and leadership style.
- Her priorities when obligations conflict.
- Her relationship to royal tradition.
- Her combat identity and evolving powers.
- Her closeness with companions.
- Her final answer to the Crown’s power.

Lyra must remain recognizably authored rather than becoming a blank avatar. Her values may vary, but her core empathy, determination, and responsibility should remain legible.

### 7.2 Core Companion Ensemble

The full vision includes a core party representing different species, histories, philosophies, and combat functions. Existing concepts include:

#### Erynn “Eryx” Vexx — Felidae Scout

A fast, guarded outcast whose sarcasm protects a strong moral line. Her history involves refusing an order to kill a prisoner, but the full political and personal context remains open. Her arc should explore conscience, exile, trust, and whether belonging requires obedience. Her play identity centers on speed, precision, risk, evasion, and opportunistic damage.

#### Brimble Toadsworth — Anura Guardian

A gentle survivor whose home was consumed by void-touched waters. His philosophy and warmth should coexist with grief, anger, and survivor’s guilt. His arc should explore stewardship, memory, renewal, and the limits of endurance. His play identity centers on protection, restoration, control, and turning danger into safety.

#### Drakkor Ashveil — Drakonid Breaker

A proud warrior who believes he failed the last people he was sworn to protect. His formality and bravado conceal fear of repeating that failure. His arc should explore honor, extinction, vulnerability, legacy, and the difference between protection and domination. His play identity centers on pressure, armor breaking, fire, commitment, and controlled devastation.

#### Pip — Construct Support

A curious hovering construct whose cheerful literalism masks uncertainty about origin and purpose. Pip’s arc should explore personhood, created purpose, chosen purpose, memory, and whether care can be programmed or freely given. Their play identity centers on repair, enhancement, analysis, adaptation, and recovery from failure.

These identities may be renamed, redesigned, expanded, or reconceived, but the product must preserve the ensemble functions they represent: contrasting cultures, emotional wounds, distinct philosophies of power, and complementary combat possibilities.

### 7.3 Companion Content Minimums

Every core companion must have:

- A compelling introduction and reason to join.
- A continuing stake in the main conflict.
- A personal quest with multiple stages.
- A relationship arc with Lyra.
- Meaningful relationships with other companions.
- Unique reactions to major decisions and locations.
- A distinct combat role with more than one viable build direction.
- A signature ability and a transformative evolution.
- A crisis or decision that tests the character’s stated beliefs.
- A resolved or intentionally transformed end state.
- Content while inactive so reserve characters do not feel absent from the story.

### 7.4 Relationships

Relationships must be driven by attention, shared experience, dialogue, decisions, and personal quest outcomes. A single hidden approval number must not be the sole expression of relationship depth.

The game may support friendship, rivalry, mentorship, familial bonds, romance, or other forms of intimacy. Exact romance scope remains open. Any romance included must:

- Be optional.
- Respect character agency and compatibility.
- Avoid making friendship feel like a failed romance.
- Develop across the journey rather than unlock through gifts alone.
- Account for the story’s power dynamics and emotional stakes.

### 7.5 NPCs and Factions

Recurring non-player characters must include:

- A mentor or military advisor who can challenge Lyra.
- Merchants and craftspeople with identities beyond their services.
- Researchers or healers who help interpret the Void.
- Historians, dissidents, and ordinary citizens who contest official narratives.
- Regional leaders with legitimate but conflicting priorities.
- Antagonists with goals that are coherent from their perspective.
- Civilians whose changing lives make consequences tangible.

Existing concepts such as Commander Reyes, Merchant Zara, Torvin, Dr. Elara, and Corvus may remain, merge, change, or expand.

## 8. World Requirements

### 8.1 Setting

The Stellar Sovereignty is a network of inhabited worlds, stations, frontier settlements, ancient sites, and unstable cosmic routes connected politically and symbolically by the Celestial Crown. The setting must make room for:

- Multiple civilizations with histories older than or independent of the Sovereignty.
- Tension between capital and frontier.
- Science and Crown power that may be interpreted differently across cultures.
- Ecological consequences of Void corruption.
- Trade, migration, faith, governance, and ordinary life.
- Ruins and records that complicate accepted history.
- Places of beauty and safety as well as danger.

### 8.2 The Voidborn

The Voidborn must be more than generic monsters. Their nature should remain initially uncertain and may encompass entities, an ecosystem, a collective, a force, transformed beings, or multiple phenomena grouped under one name.

The full story must ultimately address:

- Why the incursion occurred.
- How the Void relates to the Crown.
- Whether all Voidborn share intent.
- What corruption changes and whether it can be healed.
- What victory, containment, coexistence, or transformation would mean.

The final answer may preserve cosmic mystery, but it must resolve the practical and emotional stakes of the campaign.

### 8.3 Celestial Crown and Shards

The Crown is simultaneously:

- A source of extraordinary power.
- A symbol of rulership.
- A historical artifact with contested origins.
- A driver of progression.
- A source of temptation, obligation, and potential harm.

Each recovered shard must matter as a story event and a play event. Shards should not feel interchangeable. They may carry memories, principles, powers, costs, personalities, cultural claims, or other distinct properties. The number and exact structure remain open.

### 8.4 Regions

The campaign must include multiple substantial regions, each with:

- A strong visual, ecological, cultural, and musical identity.
- A local conflict connected to the central crisis.
- A community whose needs are understandable.
- Exploration spaces and at least one major destination.
- New enemies, hazards, resources, and strategic lessons.
- Optional stories and discoveries.
- A regional climax and lasting consequence.
- Reasons to revisit after the main objective.

Candidate concepts include:

- A luminous capital and its damaged orbital infrastructure.
- A drowned Anura world transformed by Void-touched tides.
- A Felidae colony or trade network shaped by secrecy, law, and exile.
- A Drakonid ash realm built around inheritance, heat, and extinction.
- Construct ruins or a machine archive tied to Pip’s origin.
- A fractured stellar gate network.
- A frontier where reality is visibly failing.
- The Crown’s place of origin.
- A final threshold within or beyond the Void.

These are concept spaces, not a closed list.

### 8.5 Home Base: Nova Prime

Nova Prime is the emotional and functional center of the game. It must:

- Begin in a state that communicates what was lost and what remains worth saving.
- Contain memorable residents, services, private spaces, civic spaces, and secrets.
- Change visually and socially as the player progresses.
- Reflect rescued people, alliances, investments, and consequences.
- Host companion scenes, celebrations, grief, debate, and preparation.
- Provide optional activities that deepen attachment without becoming mandatory chores.
- Remain easy to navigate as it grows.
- Face at least one threat or crisis that makes the player’s investment consequential.

Possible districts include royal, residential, market, military, research, garden, transit, and hospitality spaces. Their final arrangement and scale remain open.

### 8.6 Traversal and World Structure

The world may be structured as connected regions, a navigable overworld, mission destinations, a hub-and-spoke network, or a hybrid. Regardless of format:

- The player must understand where they can go and why.
- Travel must communicate scale and discovery without wasting time.
- New traversal options must unlock meaningful access.
- Revisiting areas must reveal changes, new routes, or new context.
- Fast travel must become available when repeated travel no longer adds value.
- Required and optional destinations must be distinguishable without erasing curiosity.

## 9. Exploration and Level Requirements

### 9.1 Exploration Principles

- Curiosity must be rewarded consistently.
- Navigation challenges must arise from readable spaces, landmarks, maps, and learned rules.
- Environmental storytelling must reveal culture, history, and recent events.
- Secrets should vary in type and significance.
- Backtracking should provide new meaning or access.
- Exploration difficulty must not depend on visual ambiguity alone.

### 9.2 Location Types

The full game should include a varied mix of:

- Social hubs and settlements.
- Wilderness or planetary environments.
- Dungeons and hostile facilities.
- Ruins and historical sites.
- Ships, stations, gates, or transit spaces.
- Companion-specific destinations.
- Optional challenge spaces.
- Story set pieces with altered rules or unusual presentation.

### 9.3 Dungeons

Every major dungeon must:

- Have a distinct premise and spatial identity.
- Introduce or deepen a small set of understandable mechanics.
- Develop those mechanics through variation and combination.
- Include opportunities for combat, discovery, story, and recovery.
- Provide shortcuts or checkpoints appropriate to its length.
- Build toward a clear climax.
- Avoid filler encounters and repeated rooms without purpose.
- Remain navigable after interruption or return.

The existing eight-room dungeon concept may become one dungeon, a structural prototype, or an inspiration rather than a universal format.

### 9.4 Puzzles and Hazards

- Puzzles must test observation, deduction, sequencing, spatial reasoning, party capabilities, or world knowledge.
- Required puzzles must support hints or alternate assistance.
- Optional puzzles may be more demanding and offer proportionate rewards.
- Hazards must telegraph rules before severe punishment.
- Party abilities may create alternate solutions where appropriate.
- Puzzle solutions must not depend on inaccessible color distinctions, audio alone, or extremely precise timing without alternatives.

### 9.5 Encounters in Space

Enemy placement and encounter initiation must support anticipation and choice where appropriate. The game may use visible enemies, authored ambushes, random pressure, or hybrids, but must avoid making exploration feel arbitrarily interrupted.

Players should be able to:

- Read escalating danger.
- Prepare for known threats.
- Avoid, engage, manipulate, or gain advantage over some encounters.
- Understand when an encounter is mandatory.
- Recover from defeat without excessive repetition.

## 10. Combat Requirements

### 10.1 Combat Experience

Combat must create the feeling of commanding a coordinated team under pressure. It should reward understanding of character roles, enemy intent, timing, resource use, status effects, positioning or targeting rules, and skill combinations.

The exact structure—pure turn-based, active-time, timeline-based, positional, or a hybrid—remains open. The final model must satisfy:

- Clear cause and effect.
- Time to make informed decisions at the chosen accessibility setting.
- Meaningful differences among speed, initiative, and interruption.
- Low downtime between decisions.
- Strong party identity and synergy.
- Reliable preview of costs, targets, and likely outcomes.
- Tactical depth without requiring opaque formulas.

### 10.2 Core Actions

The final combat model must support the conceptual functions of:

- Reliable basic actions.
- Character-defining skills.
- Defensive or mitigating choices.
- Consumable or limited-use tools.
- Party reconfiguration or reserve participation.
- Analysis of enemies and conditions.
- High-impact abilities earned through play.
- Contextual actions required by specific encounters.

The labels, control scheme, resource names, and exact action economy remain open.

### 10.3 Party Structure

- The player must control or meaningfully direct a multi-character party.
- Active and reserve characters must both retain strategic value.
- Party composition must create tradeoffs rather than a single dominant answer.
- Required story characters must not make the party nonfunctional.
- Swapping, reserve support, or equivalent flexibility must prevent one poor pre-battle choice from invalidating an encounter.
- All core companions must remain viable through the final campaign.

The exact active party size remains open.

### 10.4 Resources

Combat resources must:

- Encourage varied action rather than repetitive strongest-skill use.
- Recover at rates that support experimentation.
- Create meaningful short-term and long-term decisions.
- Be explained clearly.
- Avoid making common encounters feel punitive to engage.

Potential resources include health, skill energy, momentum, cooldowns, charges, overdrive, bond, stance, or character-specific systems. Not all are required.

### 10.5 Synergy and Resonance

Party synergy is a core requirement. Combined effects may arise from:

- Elemental interactions.
- Damage-type interactions.
- Status setup and payoff.
- Sequential abilities.
- Defensive coordination.
- Relationship-based team actions.
- Position or targeting combinations.
- Evolution states.

Named Resonance attacks, Harmony Chains, or other forms are viable. Synergy must be discoverable, previewable once learned, and broad enough to support multiple party combinations.

### 10.6 Weaknesses, Resistances, and Status

- Enemies may have physical, elemental, behavioral, positional, or phase-specific vulnerabilities.
- Discovery must be possible through observation, analysis, experimentation, or prior knowledge.
- Once discovered, information must remain available.
- Resistances and immunities must be communicated before they repeatedly waste player actions.
- Status effects must have readable duration, strength, and interaction rules.
- Boss immunity must not nullify entire character identities; bosses should transform or limit control effects rather than simply ignore most of them.

### 10.7 Enemy Design

Enemy families must:

- Express the ecology, culture, technology, or corruption of their region.
- Have readable silhouettes and intentions.
- Teach mechanics through low-risk exposure before complex combinations.
- Work in compositions that create tactical questions.
- Include variants that change behavior, not only statistics.
- Avoid excessive health or damage as a substitute for design.

### 10.8 Bosses

Every major boss must include:

- Narrative stakes and a reason this opponent matters.
- A strong visual and audio identity.
- Readable telegraphs and consistent rules.
- Multiple phases, escalating states, or equivalent dramatic development where appropriate.
- More than one viable tactical response.
- Recovery opportunities or fallback strategies.
- Mechanics connected to the region, story, or companion arc.
- A climax that tests learned skills without requiring unexplained knowledge.
- A meaningful aftermath and reward.

Bosses may incorporate arena changes, summons, breakable defenses, environmental threats, negotiation, rescue objectives, survival conditions, or nonlethal resolutions. Void Sentinel Kael, the Drowned Matriarch, and Ash Tyrant Ignis are valid concepts but not mandatory final forms.

### 10.9 Defeat and Recovery

- Defeat must communicate what happened and support a useful next decision.
- Retry friction must be low.
- Players must not lose large amounts of unrecoverable progress.
- Difficulty assistance may be offered after repeated failure without shaming the player.
- Retreat, preparation, alternate objectives, or strategy changes should remain valid where fiction permits.

## 11. Progression Requirements

### 11.1 Progression Philosophy

Progression must provide:

- Increased capability.
- New strategic possibilities.
- Visible character growth.
- Narrative recognition.
- Player expression.
- Reasons to revisit prior assumptions and locations.

Numerical growth alone is insufficient.

### 11.2 Character Growth

Each playable character must have:

- A stable identity that remains understandable.
- Multiple viable directions of specialization.
- Regular access to new or transformed abilities.
- Choices that can be reconsidered without severe punishment.
- Growth tied to both battle experience and personal development.
- A final form capable of participating meaningfully in endgame content.

The exact use of levels, skill trees, roles, stances, loadouts, or other models remains open.

### 11.3 Evolution

Evolution is a signature feature and must be:

- Mechanically significant.
- Visually transformative.
- Narratively earned.
- Accompanied by a memorable presentation.
- Integrated with character identity rather than functioning as a generic promotion.
- Expressive enough to alter builds or tactics.

Lyra’s evolution is tied to Crown Shards and her changing relationship to power. Companion evolutions are tied to personal arcs, relationships, and decisive trials. Existing conceptual stages—Crown Bearer, Starforged, Celestial Ascendant, Phantom Queen, Leviathan Sovereign, Elder Wyrm, and Omega Core—may remain, change, branch, or become one option among several.

Evolution paths may be linear, branching, reversible, conditional, or choice-driven. The final form should preserve future narrative and mechanical possibilities rather than declare that growth has permanently ended.

### 11.4 Equipment and Buildcraft

Equipment must support meaningful build decisions and character fantasy. Potential categories include weapons, armor, accessories, implants, relics, and character-specific artifacts.

The final system must:

- Make upgrades understandable.
- Avoid frequent inventory clutter.
- Distinguish interesting effects from simple stat increases.
- Make newly found items easy to compare.
- Support multiple builds per character.
- Preserve the relevance or identity of special equipment.
- Provide safe ways to experiment.
- Avoid requiring excessive farming for main-story viability.

Rarity tiers, enhancement, material fusion, transcendence, and evolving Crown Relics are valid concepts. Their exact number, naming, and economy remain open.

### 11.5 Items and Crafting

Crafting or upgrading must:

- Connect exploration rewards to player goals.
- Provide clear recipes, outcomes, and resource costs.
- Support intentional pursuit rather than pure randomness.
- Avoid excessive intermediate materials.
- Preserve the excitement of direct discovery.
- Include catch-up paths for players who change builds.

Potential material concepts include scrap metal, bio gel, Void essence, stellar crystal, dragon scale, and celestial material. These are thematic options, not fixed requirements.

### 11.6 Economy

The economy must:

- Provide understandable sources and sinks.
- Support preparation without demanding repetitive grinding.
- Keep shops relevant across the campaign.
- Make rare purchases feel aspirational but attainable.
- Prevent accidental spending from permanently harming progression.
- Scale with the player’s access to regions and services.
- Reward exploration and quests without trivializing all purchases.

Currency, barter, faction access, crafting exchange, and region-specific economies may coexist if each serves a clear purpose.

### 11.7 Rewards

Rewards may include:

- New abilities or ability variants.
- Equipment and materials.
- Relationship scenes.
- Lore and secrets.
- Home-base residents or improvements.
- Traversal access.
- Cosmetic expression.
- Alternate quest resolutions.
- Strategic information.
- Currency or consumables.

Major rewards must be previewed enough to support motivation but may retain surprise in their exact form.

## 12. Quest and Content Requirements

### 12.1 Main Quests

Main quests must:

- Advance the central conflict or Lyra’s transformation.
- Introduce meaningful new contexts, mechanics, or decisions.
- Maintain clear objectives and stakes.
- Include character participation rather than treating the party as silent equipment.
- Produce visible consequences.

### 12.2 Companion Quests

Companion quests must:

- Center the companion rather than make Lyra solve everything for them.
- Connect personal history to present choices.
- Include interaction with the companion’s culture, relationships, or consequences.
- Culminate in a decision, confrontation, sacrifice, reconciliation, or transformation.
- Affect evolution and epilogue outcomes without reducing the arc to a completion checkbox.

### 12.3 Side Quests

## 17. Art and Presentation Requirements

### 17.1 Visual Identity

The game must use a **high-resolution 2D pixel-art aesthetic modeled on the visual language of Final Fantasy IV, presented as a premium 2026 remaster**.

This is the required visual direction, not one option among several. The game must preserve the defining qualities of a classic 16-bit console RPG:

- Top-down exploration with compact, tile-based environments.
- Small but expressive character sprites with heroic proportions.
- Side-view or similarly staged combat compositions that clearly present the full party and opposing forces.
- Strong silhouettes, selective detail, deliberate color ramps, and visible pixel craftsmanship.
- Illustrated character portraits and dramatic enemy artwork that expand beyond the scale of exploration sprites.
- Ornamental fantasy interfaces, readable iconography, and framed dialogue presentation.
- Stylized spell effects and transformation sequences that favor graphic impact over realism.

The 2026 remaster treatment must elevate that foundation through:

- Higher-resolution source artwork with more facial, costume, material, and environmental detail.
- More animation frames and smoother transitions while retaining intentional pixel-art timing.
- Modern lighting, atmosphere, particles, layering, and screen composition that do not obscure the pixel-art foundation.
- Widescreen presentation composed intentionally rather than exposing empty space around layouts designed for a narrower frame.
- Contemporary readability, accessibility, display scaling, and visual feedback.
- Cinematic staging for major story moments without abandoning the established 2D visual language.

The game must not use polygonal characters or environments as its normal player-facing visual style. Three-dimensional tools may support production, reference, previsualization, or effects only when the final visible result remains consistent with authored 2D pixel art.

The art direction must be recognizably inspired by the era and visual grammar of Final Fantasy IV without copying its characters, creatures, locations, interface assets, compositions, or other protected expression. Stellar Princesses must establish its own science-fantasy identity.

### 17.2 Pixel-Art Standards

- Pixel placement must appear intentional at every gameplay scale. Artwork must not resemble low-resolution images that were merely enlarged or filtered.
- Sprite pixels must remain crisp, stable, and consistently scaled during movement, camera motion, effects, and interface transitions.
- The game must use a coherent base pixel density and proportion system across characters, environments, props, and effects.
- Higher detail must come from richer pixel art, additional animation, layered composition, and selective high-resolution illustration—not from smoothing the pixels away.
- Nearest-neighbor visual clarity must be preserved for gameplay sprites and tiles.
- Sub-pixel effects, lighting, or camera movement may be used only when they do not produce shimmer, crawling edges, blurred sprites, or inconsistent pixel scale.
- Rotated or scaled pixel-art elements must be redrawn, constrained, or presented in a way that preserves visual integrity.
- Palette design must use deliberate ramps, hue shifts, contrast control, and regional color scripts rather than unrestricted or noisy color selection.
- Outlines may vary by material, lighting, or character importance, but must follow a consistent art-direction rule.
- Repeated tiles and modular environment pieces must include enough variation, overlays, and authored landmarks to avoid obvious visual repetition.
- Pixel-art quality must be judged at native gameplay scale and at every supported display scale.

### 17.3 Resolution and Detail Hierarchy

The product must define distinct visual tiers so that additional resolution serves the player instead of creating inconsistent detail:

1. **Exploration sprites** must clearly communicate identity, facing, movement, mood, and important equipment at normal gameplay scale.
2. **Combat sprites** must be larger or more detailed than exploration sprites and support distinctive poses, attacks, reactions, status conditions, and evolutions.
3. **Portraits and dialogue art** must communicate facial expression, species traits, costume detail, and emotional nuance beyond what sprites can show.
4. **Boss and set-piece art** must receive the highest gameplay-art detail and animation emphasis.
5. **Key illustrations** may use substantially higher resolution for introductions, transformations, major revelations, and endings while preserving pixel-art or pixel-informed rendering.

Characters and environments must not compete at the same detail level. Playable characters, enemies, exits, hazards, and interactable objects must remain immediately legible against richer backgrounds.

### 17.4 Character Art

- Every major character must be recognizable by silhouette, palette, costume structure, motion, and portrait treatment.
- Lyra’s design must combine royal identity with visible progression from protected heir to capable cosmic warrior.
- Each companion must have a distinct body shape, stance, movement rhythm, equipment language, and species-specific animation.
- Species design must communicate individual character and culture rather than functioning as a human body with a single animal or mechanical feature added.
- Costumes must merge science-fiction function with ceremonial fantasy design.
- Exploration, combat, portrait, evolution, and cinematic depictions of the same character must remain consistent.
- Character palettes must remain distinguishable across common backgrounds, status effects, and party combinations.
- Equipment progression may alter visible details where it strengthens reward and identity, but characters must remain recognizable.
- Damage, healing, guarding, status, victory, defeat, and emotional states must have visual expression.
- Important NPCs must receive enough visual specificity to be recognized without relying solely on labels.
- Background NPCs may use a more modular art approach but must still reflect regional culture, occupation, age, and social variety.

### 17.5 Character Portraits and Expressions

- Every core companion and recurring major character must have dedicated portrait art.
- Portraits must support a useful range of expressions rather than one neutral image for all dialogue.
- Expression changes must correspond to the emotional intent of the scene and must not cycle excessively.
- Portrait framing, lighting, and pose may change for exceptional story states, injuries, transformations, or revelations.
- Minor characters may use fewer expressions, but their portrait treatment must remain consistent with the main cast.
- Portraits must not conflict with the proportions, costume, palette, or species traits established by gameplay sprites.

### 17.6 Animation Quality

Animation must retain the readability and authored timing of classic sprite animation while meeting modern expectations for character life and combat impact.

#### Exploration Animation

- Playable characters must have directional movement animation with clear foot placement and no visible sliding.
- Idle characters must display subtle life through breathing, posture changes, species traits, equipment motion, or situational behavior.
- Interactions must use appropriate facing, anticipation, and response rather than relying entirely on static sprites.
- Companions and NPCs must not appear frozen during active scenes.
- Environmental animation must include purposeful movement such as water, vegetation, lights, machinery, weather, crowds, or cosmic phenomena.

#### Combat Animation

- Every playable character must have distinct idle, ready, basic attack, skill, defend, hit, critical, status, defeat, recovery, and victory states.
- Signature abilities must have unique anticipation, action, impact, and recovery beats.
- Animation timing must make attack cause, target response, and result easy to follow.
- Enemies must telegraph dangerous actions through poses, motion, effects, or arena changes before the outcome occurs.
- Reactions must reflect force, damage type, weakness, resistance, interruption, and defeat where appropriate.
- Repeated actions must include enough variation to avoid appearing mechanical during normal play.
- Bosses must receive bespoke animation appropriate to their narrative and mechanical importance.

#### Dialogue and Cinematic Animation

- Story scenes must use blocking, facing, movement, pauses, portrait expression, effects, and environmental response to communicate emotion.
- Major scenes must not consist solely of stationary sprites exchanging text when the fiction calls for physical action or reaction.
- Subtle character animation must support quiet scenes without creating constant distracting motion.
- Evolutions and major revelations must use bespoke animation and composition rather than ordinary combat effects enlarged on screen.

Animation smoothness must not erase purposeful holds, anticipation, or snappy impact. Additional frames are required where they improve expression, motion clarity, and production value; frame count alone is not the quality target.

### 17.7 Environment Art

- Every region must have a unique palette, architecture, material language, atmosphere, environmental motion, and landmark strategy.
- Locations must combine classic tile-based readability with richer layers, depth, variation, and lived-in detail.
- Environments must communicate how people live, travel, work, worship, govern, celebrate, and respond to danger.
- Science-fiction devices and fantasy structures must share a coherent shape language instead of appearing as unrelated genres placed together.
- Interiors must have functional spatial logic and visual specificity appropriate to their occupants.
- Natural spaces must show distinct ecology rather than palette-swapped terrain.
- Foreground layers, elevation, shadows, weather, atmospheric effects, and parallax may add depth but must never hide objectives or collision boundaries.
- Interactive elements and hazards must be readable without looking disconnected from the world.
- Environments must support environmental storytelling.
- Home-base changes must be visually apparent.
- Nova Prime must become warmer, busier, more restored, and more culturally varied as the player’s alliances and achievements grow.
- Void influence must have understandable visual stages or forms and must alter places in ways that are beautiful, threatening, and narratively informative.
- Important destinations must have establishing compositions and memorable landmarks.
- Secret areas must be discoverable through visual logic rather than indistinguishable walls or arbitrary interaction.

### 17.8 Combat Composition and Readability

- Combat must evoke the staged clarity and dramatic side-view presentation associated with classic 16-bit party RPGs while using modern framing and effects.
- The party and enemy group must remain readable simultaneously.
- Character spacing, scale, and staging must preserve the importance of the full ensemble.
- Backgrounds must represent the current location and support atmosphere without competing with combatants.
- Allies, enemies, targets, ranges, intent, hazards, and status must remain distinguishable.
- Effects must communicate timing and outcome without obscuring subsequent decisions.
- Critical events must receive proportionate emphasis.
- Damage and healing feedback must be understandable without relying on numbers alone.
- Boss scale must feel imposing without placing essential information outside the visible composition.
- Camera movement may emphasize attacks and reactions but must return promptly to a stable tactical view.
- Common actions must remain visually efficient; high-impact presentation must be reserved for abilities and events that deserve it.
- Players must be able to shorten or accelerate repeated combat presentation without losing tactical information.
- Reduced-effects settings must preserve all tactical information.

### 17.9 Visual Effects and Lighting

- Effects must be authored in a pixel-art or pixel-compatible style.
- Magic, technology, and Void power must each have distinct motion, color, shape, and impact languages.
- Lyra’s stellar power must emphasize radiance, constellations, crowns, orbiting forms, and controlled celestial geometry.
- Void effects must emphasize broken continuity, impossible depth, corruption, absence, and unstable form without relying only on purple-and-black coloring.
- Elemental effects must remain identifiable when color is unavailable.
- Lighting must reinforce time, place, emotion, and power while preserving sprite readability.
- Bloom, glow, distortion, blur, chromatic effects, and particles must be restrained enough to preserve crisp pixel forms.
- Screen shake, flashes, hit stops, and full-screen effects must be proportionate, configurable, and accessible.
- Effects must have clear start, impact, and resolution states.
- Persistent hazards and status effects must remain readable after spectacular effects conclude.

### 17.10 Evolution Presentation

Evolution is the visual signature of Stellar Princesses and must receive the product’s highest art and animation priority.

- Every evolution must visibly preserve the character while changing silhouette, costume, aura, animation, and power expression.
- Transformation designs must reflect the character’s resolved conflict or chosen identity, not merely add armor and glow.
- Lyra’s progression must increasingly merge regal and cosmic imagery without losing her humanity.
- Companion evolutions must remain culturally and personally specific.
- Evolution presentation must include a bespoke sequence, a changed combat presence, and persistent visual recognition afterward.
- Final forms must feel extraordinary beside ordinary party states without making earlier designs appear disposable.
- Alternate or branching evolutions, if supported, must be equally finished and thematically coherent.
- Evolution effects must remain comprehensible with reduced flashing and reduced-motion settings.

### 17.11 Cinematic Presentation

Major story moments, companion turning points, evolutions, boss introductions, regional reveals, and endings require elevated presentation within the high-resolution 2D style.

- Cinematics may combine animated sprites, portraits, high-resolution pixel illustrations, camera composition, environmental animation, and effects.
- Cinematic artwork must look like a richer expression of the game rather than a switch to an unrelated style.
- Establishing shots must communicate scale beyond the immediate play area.
- Character staging and reaction shots must make emotional causality clear.
- Major actions described in dialogue must be shown when they are central to the scene.
- Quiet scenes must receive the same compositional care as spectacle.
- Skipping, advancing, pausing, and reviewing story presentation must respect player control.
- Spectacle must support emotion and clarity rather than replace them.

### 17.12 Interface Art

- The interface must evoke ornate science-fantasy royalty through frames, celestial motifs, material accents, and typography while remaining fast and readable.
- Interface ornament must never reduce text space, obscure focus, or create unnecessary visual noise.
- Menus must visually belong to the same world as the environments and character art.
- Pixel-art icons must remain legible at all supported scales.
- Character portraits, equipment icons, status symbols, elemental symbols, and map markers must use one coherent visual system.
- Selection, disabled, equipped, compared, upgraded, rare, and dangerous states must be distinguishable without color alone.
- Dialogue boxes must support portraits and emotional scenes without covering critical staging.
- The interface must scale cleanly for modern displays without mixing crisp gameplay art with blurry or inconsistently scaled UI elements.

### 17.13 Art Consistency and Acceptance

The art direction is successful only when:

- A gameplay image is immediately identifiable as a modern, premium interpretation of a classic 16-bit party RPG.
- The image is unmistakably Stellar Princesses rather than a reproduction of its influences.
- Exploration, combat, portraits, cinematics, effects, and interface appear to belong to one product.
- Increased resolution adds expression and specificity without losing pixel-art discipline.
- Animation feels materially richer than a 1990s production while preserving strong poses and readable timing.
- Characters remain readable against every region and combat background.
- The visual hierarchy remains functional with reduced effects and accessibility settings.
- No player-facing scene depends on polygonal rendering to achieve its intended final appearance.
- Representative final-quality content exists for Lyra, every core companion, a common enemy, a boss, Nova Prime, a field region, a dungeon, combat effects, an evolution, dialogue, and all major interface contexts before the overall visual target is considered validated.

### 17.14 Explicit Visual Exclusions

The final product must not:

- Use a low-detail prototype aesthetic as the finished art style.
- Present normal gameplay as conventional polygonal 3D.
- Mix smooth vector characters, painted backgrounds, raw procedural primitives, and pixel sprites without a unifying pixel-art treatment.
- Apply smoothing filters that blur or round off gameplay pixels.
- Use automated upscaling artifacts as final authored detail.
- Rely on palette swaps as the primary distinction among regions, cultures, or major enemies.
- Use excessive particles, bloom, darkness, or foreground obstruction that reduces playability.
- Copy recognizable assets, compositions, character designs, creatures, or interface elements from Final Fantasy IV or any other reference work.

## 24. Risks and Product Guardrails

### 24.1 Scope Risk

The vision includes narrative branching, companion depth, multiple regions, deep progression, and substantial presentation requirements. Scope decisions must protect, in order:

1. A complete and coherent campaign.
2. Lyra and the core companion arcs.
3. The core combat and exploration experience.
4. Distinct regions and bosses.
5. Home-base consequence and ending reactivity.
6. Optional breadth.

Content volume must be reduced before essential arcs are made shallow.

### 24.2 Systems Complexity Risk

Combat, equipment, crafting, relationships, home-base growth, and branching narrative can overwhelm players. Every system must justify:

- The decisions it creates.
- The fantasy it expresses.
- The feedback it provides.
- Its interaction with the rest of the product.

Systems that duplicate another system’s purpose should be combined, simplified, or omitted.

### 24.3 Tonal Risk

The premise combines princess fantasy, cute alien companions, political questions, grief, and cosmic horror. The tone must allow contrast without treating sincerity as embarrassment. Humor should release tension, not erase consequence.

### 24.4 Representation Risk

Alien species and cultures must not map lazily onto real-world stereotypes. Cultural design requires internal diversity, material logic, and sensitivity review where real-world inspiration is substantial.

### 24.5 Choice Risk

Choices must not be advertised as more consequential than they are. Where production scope limits branching, the game should provide honest local, relational, thematic, or epilogue consequences rather than cosmetic dialogue swaps presented as radically different paths.

### 25.2 Narrative

- What is the Crown’s true origin?
- Did the Sovereignty misunderstand, conceal, or exploit that origin?
- What do the Voidborn want, and are they unified?
- Can corruption be reversed?
- What forms can Lyra’s final political choice take?
- How much can regional outcomes diverge?
- Which relationships, including romance if any, are supported?

### 25.3 Combat

- What balance of turn-based deliberation and real-time urgency best serves the audience?
- Does combat use explicit positioning, abstract formation, or targeting alone?
- How do reserve companions participate?
- How are combo discoveries taught and recorded?
- How much character-specific resource complexity is sustainable?
- Can major conflicts end through objectives other than defeating all opponents?

### 25.4 Progression

- Are evolutions fixed, branching, or shaped by player values?
- How much equipment randomness supports discovery without undermining intent?
- Which forms of respecialization best respect player time?
- How do relationship and battle progression interact without forcing unwanted party use?
- What persists into optional postgame or New Game Plus?

### 25.5 World and Content

- How many regions can receive the required depth?
- Which cultures and factions are essential to the first complete campaign?
- How extensively can Nova Prime change?
- What optional content best reinforces theme rather than merely increasing volume?
- Which mysteries should remain open for future stories?

### 25.6 Presentation

- How much cinematic staging is needed for the emotional target?
- What degree of voice performance best fits scope and identity?
- Should music be authored, adaptive, procedural, or hybrid?
- How can transformations feel spectacular while preserving accessibility settings?

Open questions should be resolved through concept development, prototypes, representative content, playtesting, production constraints, and thematic coherence. Resolving one question must not unnecessarily eliminate future expansion paths.

## 26. Full-Vision Acceptance Criteria

The product vision is complete when all of the following are true:

- A player can begin a new game, learn the essential experience, complete the full campaign, make a consequential final decision, and view an epilogue without external guidance.
- Lyra has a coherent, player-shaped arc from sheltered heir to self-defined leader.
- Every core companion can be recruited, developed, evolved, and brought to a meaningful conclusion.
- The central mystery of the Crown and the practical stakes of the Voidborn are resolved sufficiently for the campaign to stand alone.
- Multiple distinct regions provide meaningful exploration, local stories, mechanical development, and climactic encounters.
- Nova Prime functions as a useful home base and visibly reflects the journey.
- Combat remains readable, strategic, and viable across supported party compositions and difficulty choices.
- Progression regularly creates new decisions and expressive possibilities.
- Major bosses are fair, memorable, and connected to the game’s narrative and mechanical lessons.
- Main quests, companion quests, and optional content meet their stated quality requirements.
- Controls, interface, tutorials, maps, saving, difficulty, and accessibility support full completion.
- Art and audio form a cohesive identity across characters, locations, combat, transformation, and story.
- The game is stable, preserves player progress, and meets release quality requirements.
- The base release feels complete while leaving the universe open to future stories, systems, and interpretations.

## 27. Product North Star

When priorities conflict, choose the direction that best delivers this outcome:

> The player finishes Stellar Princesses feeling that they crossed a wondrous and wounded galaxy with people they came to love, earned powers that changed both play and identity, protected a home that visibly grew around them, and made a final choice about power that belonged to their version of Lyra.
